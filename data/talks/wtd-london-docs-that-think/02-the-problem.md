# Problems for technical writers

**Stale content** — Code ships. Docs don't follow.

**Spread thin** — Smaller & smaller teams, large amounts to content, mundainity.

**Style inconsistency** — Different writers, different voices, no enforcement.

**Changelog tedium** — Every release, someone has to parse every commit.

**Engineer bottleneck** — Custom components, config changes, dependency upgrades all need a dev.

**No tooling feedback loop** — Docs live outside CI. Nobody catches issues early.

---

# Problems for users

**Making sense of huge volumes of content** - Too much to read, constantly changing.

**Untested instructions** — "Follow these steps" but nobody checks if they actually work.

**Not built for AI consumers** — Users paste your docs into ChatGPT, but the format isn't optimised for it.

---

# Why use AI?

- More time for the things you're good at
- Work like a developer
- Your customers are using AI

# From the horse's mouth

> why should an AI sceptic technical writer consider using ai tooling

Good question — and worth being honest about the tensions in it. Here's the case, without overselling it:

The strongest arguments:

- Speed on the mundane. Technical writing involves a lot of work that isn't writing — reformatting, restructuring, generating boilerplate, drafting changelog entries, producing first-pass API docs from a spec. AI handles this adequately. If you're skeptical of AI, the goal isn't to love it; it's to not spend 40 minutes on something that takes 4.
- A draft is easier to critique than a blank page. Even a mediocre AI draft gives you something to push against. For technical writers who know exactly what wrong looks like, that friction is useful.
- Consistency checking at scale. Across large doc sets — terminology drift, inconsistent tone, missing steps — AI can surface problems faster than a human read-through. It's grunt work.

The honest counterarguments you shouldn't dismiss:
- AI-generated technical writing tends toward the generic. It flattens voice, over-explains, and misses the specific insight that makes good docs actually useful. If your skepticism is "AI writing is worse than mine," you're probably right. The question is whether it's a net tool, not a replacement.
- It also creates a subtle risk: accepting plausible-sounding content that's technically wrong. In technical writing, that's not a style problem — it's a liability.

The reframe worth considering:
- The best use isn't "AI writes, I edit." It's "I write, AI handles the peripheral tasks." That keeps your judgment in the loop and your voice intact, while recovering time on the stuff that doesn't require either.

**Skepticism is a reasonable starting position. The question is whether it's load-bearing or just habitual.**
